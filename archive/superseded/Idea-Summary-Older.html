# Idea Summary Compiled by Draper — 17/06/2025 --- ## Idea / Product Description A mobile application that helps parents develop independence and life skills in children aged 6–17, through a structured system of tasks, gamification, and AI-powered planning. The parent describes what they want their child to learn — by voice or text. An AI agent interprets this, asks clarifying questions if needed, and generates a set of quests for the child in a structured format. The child sees their tasks in a game-like interface: a skill tree, experience points, levels, and a wish-list progress bar (a specific desired reward set by the child, fulfilled by the parent when the bar reaches 100%). The parent tracks progress, confirms completed tasks, and receives weekly development reports. The pedagogical foundation is explicit and intentional: - **Vygotsky's Zone of Proximal Development (ZPD):** tasks are calibrated to sit just above the child's current ability — challenging enough to grow, achievable enough to complete. Scaffolding levels range from full step-by-step instruction (Level 3) down to goal-only autonomy (Level 0), and are reduced as the child masters each skill. - **Ebbinghaus Forgetting Curve:** mastered skills are scheduled for spaced repetition (3 → 7 → 14 → 30 → 60 days) to ensure habits are genuinely formed, not just completed once. - **Self-Determination Theory (motivation):** the reward system is designed to gradually shift from external motivation (wish-list rewards) toward internal motivation (autonomy, mastery, recognition) — the AI agent actively prompts the parent to reduce rewards as a skill becomes habitual. The child's interface is designed to feel like a game (quests, XP, levels, skill tree, visual progress). The parent's interface is designed to feel like a development dashboard (skill health indicators, weekly reports, ZPD recommendations). --- ## Idea Origin The founder has spent over three years teaching 3D modelling to school-age children. This direct, sustained experience with children as learners — and with parents as buyers — is the primary source of the idea. The founder observed first-hand that parents struggle to know what skills to develop in their children, how to motivate them, and how to track meaningful progress. The idea emerged from this lived frustration, not from desk research. --- ## Opportunity / Problem Parents — particularly in major cities like Moscow — face a compound problem: 1. **They don't know what to teach.** There is an overwhelming amount of information about child development, skills, and education. Parents lack a clear, structured framework for deciding what their child should be learning at what age. 2. **They don't know how to motivate.** Traditional approaches (nagging, money for grades, punishment) are known to fail or to create counterproductive dynamics (external rewards displacing internal motivation). 3. **They can't track real progress.** There is no system that gives parents an honest, data-driven picture of whether their child is actually developing independence and capability — or just going through the motions. The pain is particularly acute for parents of children aged 8–12 in urban Russia/CIS, where educational investment is high but structured home-based development is largely unguided. The broader global market for parenting and edtech apps is large and growing. Direct competitors (chore apps with reward mechanics: Chores Hero, Questmo, BusyKid, Habitica) exist but share a common weakness: they are task managers with coins, not genuine development systems. None are built on an explicit pedagogical model. --- ## Target Customer **Primary:** Parents of children aged 8–12 in Russia and CIS (initially), with global expansion intended. The specific features that make this parent a buyer: - They are actively investing in their child's development (already paying for tutors, clubs, sections). - They feel the anxiety of "I don't know if I'm doing the right things." - They want to give their child real-world skills and independence, not just academic performance. - They are comfortable with mobile apps and willing to pay for tools that demonstrably work. The product also has a secondary user: the child (aged 8–12 for the core segment), whose engagement is the core retention driver. If the child does not engage, the parent cancels. The child's experience is therefore as important as the parent's. Secondary age segments (6–8 and 13–17) are noted as future roadmap — they require different interface modes but share the same pedagogical core. --- ## Secret Advantages - **3+ years of direct, paid teaching experience with school-age children** in 3D modelling. This gives the founder genuine insight into how children learn, what motivates them, what frustrates them, and how parents behave as buyers. - **Direct access to the target audience** for early interviews, prototyping, and Wizard-of-Oz testing — without needing to spend on acquisition. - **Pedagogical credibility** that pure-tech founders building in this space will lack. The application of Vygotsky's ZPD and Ebbinghaus spacing to a consumer app is not standard — it requires someone who understands both the theory and the child. --- ## Competitors Based on founder knowledge and preliminary research, the competitive landscape includes: - **Chore/task apps with reward mechanics:** Chores Hero, Questmo, ChoreSplit, TaskStars, Tibo — assign tasks, earn coins, redeem rewards. Weak on pedagogy, strong on simplicity. - **AI-generated task apps:** FamilyHero, KidTasks AI — personalised tasks by age and interest. Closer in concept, but still transactional rather than developmental. - **Financial literacy + chores:** BusyKid, Greenlight — tasks mapped to pocket money. Different value proposition (financial skills, not general independence). - **Adult habit trackers used by families:** Habitica — gamified, but not designed for children or parent-child dynamics. **Founder's assessment:** No direct competitor combines AI-powered parental setup, a genuine ZPD-based skill progression model, and a wish-list motivation mechanic in one product. The space is fragmented and shallow on pedagogy. --- ## Business Model - **Freemium subscription** paid by the parent. - Free tier: 1 child, basic task library, wish-list progress bar, streak tracking. - Paid tier (price TBD — to be validated via landing page test): AI voice agent for setup, multiple children, skill analytics, weekly development reports, ZPD recommendations. - Annual subscription with discount — standard retention mechanic. - **What will NOT be monetised:** streak freezes sold to children, in-app currency purchasable by children. These create dark patterns and carry regulatory risk in children's apps (152-FZ, COPPA, GDPR-K). - **Future B2B2C channel:** music schools, sports clubs, tutoring centres pay for a parent engagement and homework tool — low CAC acquisition channel. - **Future marketplace layer:** once the system has 2+ months of behavioural data on a child, it can recommend specific coaches and sections, with a referral commission model. This is a later-stage revenue stream, not an MVP feature. - **Geographic focus:** Russia and CIS initially; global expansion intended but deferred until localisation is resourced. --- ## Additional Notes - **Motivational system design principle:** The system must gradually dismantle its own reward structure. As a skill becomes habitual, the AI agent prompts the parent to reduce or remove the reward. This is the core philosophical differentiation from competitors — "the only app that teaches children to want to, not just to do it for coins." - **Duolingo-style streaks, adapted for children:** Streaks count completed tasks, not app opens. Streak freezes are earned through actions, not purchased. A shared "family streak" (parent must also confirm progress) increases parental engagement. - **Photo confirmation:** Photos of completed tasks are captured as an achievement archive and shared memory — not as AI verification. Parent confirmation ("Done" → "Confirm") is the verification mechanism for MVP. - **Wish-list mechanic:** Child submits a wish. Parent sets the task contribution rate (e.g. "+5% toward your wish for washing dishes after lunch"). When the bar reaches 100%, the parent fulfils the wish. This is named explicitly as a motivational anchor — concrete goal, not abstract currency. - **Social/competitive layer:** Considered but to be handled carefully. No public child leaderboards. Anonymous percentile benchmarks for parents ("your child is in the top 20% of their age group for independence") is the maximum — and not in MVP. - **AI architecture:** Voice input from parent → STT (Whisper / Yandex SpeechKit) → LLM with strict JSON schema (DeepSeek / GigaChat / Claude Haiku) with `requires_clarification` field for ambiguous inputs → structured quest cards → parent approves with one tap. Estimated AI cost: ~20–50 ₽/family/month at reasonable usage levels. - **The "talent discovery + marketplace" direction** surfaced during the interview as a potential second product. Decision taken: this is a future layer (Month 6+), not a founding premise. The daily-use habit app is the correct foundation; marketplace recommendations emerge naturally from behavioural data once it exists.
